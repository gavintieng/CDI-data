About This Project

Abstract

Our project investigates the effect of \(14\) different variables on the mean number of active, nonfederal Physicians in 1990. We aim to find a model that utilizes the most relevant factors to help determine this number. In some cases, we studied whether or not the output of variables vary based on the values of other independent variable, in which case there would be a relationship between predictors. We began by analyzing the effect that the estimated \(1990\) population, land area, and income per capita of selected counties had on the number of professionally active nonfederal physicians in \(1990\). We then investigated the effect that the estimated \(1990\) population and geographic region had on the number of professional active nonfederal physicians in \(1990\).

Motivation

Health-care rankings are not only dependent on quality of care, but also access of care. The number of health-care physicians in a city is essential for sufficient primary care. Primary care depends on the availability and the number of physicians, then the variables that influence the number of physicians becomes increasingly important in determining the quality of health-care in a city. Our project analyzes a data set that contains demographic information that may or may not affect the number of physicians in a particular county in the United States. The data looks at the effect of \(14\) different input variables in \(440\) individual counties. The goal of our regression analysis in part one is to determine which variables, if any, out of total population, land area, and income per capita are significant in predicting the true number of physicians in a given county. Similarly, in part two, we initially began by considering the effects of total population and region on the number of physicians. Later, we introduced and analyzed \(5\) more input variables: percent of the population aged \(65\) and older, the number of crimes, the percent of adults with a bachelor’s degree, percent of population in poverty, and total personal income.

Dataset

Our data came from county demographic information (CDI) from 440 of the most populous counties in the United States. The information pertains to the years 1990 and 1992.
